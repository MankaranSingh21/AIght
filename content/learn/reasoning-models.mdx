Most AI models answer immediately. You ask, they respond. The response emerges token by token, and whatever thinking happens is hidden inside the model's weights.

Reasoning models are different. They think out loud first.

## What chain-of-thought actually means

The core insight behind reasoning models is simple: for hard problems, the act of generating intermediate steps improves accuracy.

If you ask a model to solve a multi-step maths problem and it jumps straight to an answer, it's compressing a complex computation into a single leap. That leap often fails. But if you ask it to show its work — "first let me identify the variables, now compute the intermediate values, now check whether this makes sense" — the extended sequence of tokens acts as a scratchpad, and the final answer is more reliable.

This was first demonstrated in Google's 2022 paper on chain-of-thought prompting. Simply adding "Let's think step by step" to a prompt improved performance significantly on reasoning tasks. Not because those words are magic — but because they elicit a different mode of generation.

<Callout>
Chain-of-thought works because tokens are computation. More thinking tokens = more steps the model can take before committing to an answer.
</Callout>

## Inference-time compute scaling

Standard models scale by training: more data, more parameters, more GPU-hours during training.

Reasoning models introduce a different lever: **inference-time compute**. Instead of a bigger model trained for longer, you get a standard model that's allowed to think for longer at the moment you ask your question.

This has a striking implication: you can choose how much reasoning to buy. An easy question gets a brief chain-of-thought. A complex maths problem gets an extended one. The model can even restart, backtrack, and try different approaches — all before delivering its answer.

OpenAI's o1 and o3 models formalised this. They train models specifically to produce these extended reasoning chains using reinforcement learning — rewarding correct final answers regardless of the path taken.

## What happens inside a reasoning trace

When a reasoning model works through a problem, the internal trace often looks like:

- Restating the question to clarify what's being asked
- Identifying relevant constraints or given information
- Attempting a solution, then noticing a flaw
- Backtracking and trying a different approach
- Checking the answer against the original constraints
- Expressing appropriate uncertainty before committing

This isn't just theatrics. The backtracking and self-correction are functional — the model is genuinely using earlier tokens in its context to reconsider later ones.

<Pullquote>
The thinking trace isn't decoration. It's computation that happens to be visible.
</Pullquote>

## When to use reasoning models — and when not to

Reasoning models are better at:

- Mathematical and logical problems with definite correct answers
- Code debugging where the error requires multi-step diagnosis
- Scientific reasoning with explicit constraints
- Tasks where overconfidence is costly

Reasoning models are slower and more expensive than standard models. They're worse choices for:

- Simple factual lookups
- Tasks requiring speed over depth
- Creative generation where the "right" answer doesn't exist
- Tasks where the extended thinking might overcomplicate a straightforward response

A model that reasons carefully about "what's 2+2?" is wasting compute. A model that jumps straight to an answer on "prove Fermat's Last Theorem" will fail.

## The limitation no one talks about

Reasoning models are better at tasks with **verifiable correct answers**. They're less clearly better at tasks where "quality" is inherently subjective — writing, strategy, communication.

And there's a subtler problem: long reasoning chains can compound errors. If the model makes a wrong assumption in step 2, it may reason coherently from that wrong foundation all the way to a confident wrong answer. A shorter chain might have failed earlier and more visibly.

Reasoning traces are also not transparent in the way they appear. The chain-of-thought is generated by the same model that generates the answer — it's a plausible-sounding thought process, not a window into the actual computation. The model may generate a confident chain-of-thought for a wrong answer just as fluently as for a correct one.
