What if every word had an address? Not a dictionary entry, but a location in a map of meaning — where proximity meant similarity, and distance meant difference. Where "joyful" and "elated" lived a few steps apart, "cold" and "winter" were neighbors, and "photosynthesis" was somewhere far across town from "heartbreak."

That's what embeddings are. An embedding is a list of numbers — hundreds or thousands of them — that represents the meaning of a piece of text. An embedding model learned, from reading an enormous amount of text, to place similar things near each other in this space and different things farther apart. The numbers themselves are arbitrary. The distances between them are what matter.

This sounds abstract. The useful thing is that it actually works.

---

## The geometry of meaning

When researchers first found that these vectors had spatial structure — not just proximity between synonyms, but meaningful arithmetic — it was surprising enough to become something of a famous example.

Take the vector for "king." Subtract the vector for "man." Add the vector for "woman." The result lands very close to the vector for "queen." The geometry of gender, encoded in the space, applies consistently across concept pairs.

This isn't handcoded. The model learned it from patterns in text. And it holds across many kinds of relationships — capitals and countries, verb tenses, comparatives. The structure of meaning shows up as structure in space.

```python
import numpy as np

def cosine_similarity(a: list[float], b: list[float]) -> float:
    a, b = np.array(a), np.array(b)
    return float(np.dot(a, b) / (np.linalg.norm(a) * np.linalg.norm(b)))

# Embed a collection of texts
documents = [
    "the cat sat on the mat",
    "a feline rested on the rug",
    "machine learning optimizes parameters",
]
doc_embeddings = [embed(doc) for doc in documents]

# Find which document is closest to a query
query_vec = embed("a cat lying down")
scores = [cosine_similarity(query_vec, e) for e in doc_embeddings]
# scores ≈ [0.91, 0.87, 0.22]
# The first two are semantically close; the third is unrelated
```

> Keyword search finds words. Semantic search finds meaning. Embeddings are why a search for "rental car" can return results about "vehicle hire" — the query and the document live near each other even if they share no words.

## Why this matters for AI tools

Embeddings are the foundation of most of what feels "smart" about modern AI search and retrieval. When you upload a document and an AI answers questions about it accurately, something like this happened: your document was chunked and embedded, your question was embedded, and the chunks whose embeddings were closest to your question's embedding were retrieved and fed to the model.

This is why RAG works. The retrieval step — finding the right chunks — is a search through embedding space.

It's also why semantic search tools feel qualitatively different from old-school keyword matching. You can search for "something about dealing with a difficult manager" and surface a note you wrote about "navigating a tough workplace relationship" — because in embedding space, those two phrases are close.

---

## What embeddings can and can't do

**They capture semantic similarity well.** General-purpose embedding models are good at measuring whether two pieces of text are about the same thing. Specialized models exist for code, multilingual content, and long documents — each trained on the kinds of text they're meant to handle.

**They're not magic retrieval systems.** An embedding model has no special knowledge about your domain. If your documents use internal jargon that doesn't appear in the model's training data, the embeddings may not reflect your intended meaning well. Fine-tuned embedding models exist for exactly this reason.

**Dimensionality matters.** Larger embedding vectors (more numbers) can capture more nuance, but take more memory and compute to search. Most production systems balance between a few hundred and a few thousand dimensions depending on what latency and accuracy tradeoffs they're willing to make.

---

The coordinates are arbitrary. The distances are not. That gap — between the representation and the meaning it encodes — is where most of the interesting questions about what AI "understands" actually live.

For now, the practical outcome is this: text can be placed in a space where proximity means something, and that space can be searched. It's a small, strange, quietly powerful idea.
