Every generative model in production today is, by construction, a
sophisticated average. It samples from what humans have already made. When
it produces something that feels novel, the novelty is interpolative — a
plausible combination of patterns it's already seen. This is not a flaw.
It is the design.

What it means is that the model is structurally incapable of being the
first one to make a particular thing. The first sentence of a genre. The
first proof of a theorem. The first photograph that taught us to see a
city differently. The thing that hadn't yet been made, and after which the
average shifted to include it.

Originality is the bet you make against the average. It's the painter who
spends fifteen years on a way of seeing that nobody asked for, and which
later turns out to have been a door. It's the engineer who refuses the
obvious architecture because she's chasing a smaller, weirder one she
can't yet justify. It's the writer who keeps a sentence everyone tells her
to cut because she knows it's doing the load-bearing work the rest of the
paragraph won't.

The reason this matters now is that the cost of producing the average has
gone to zero. Anybody can generate the median essay, the median image, the
median pitch deck. The median is now noise. So the work that has any
chance of mattering is the work that refuses to be the median — and
deliberately so.

That's a strange place to live. It costs more. It looks wrong from the
outside more often than not. It is, by definition, the thing nobody is
asking for, until they are. And it is the one kind of work the AI tools
genuinely cannot help you do, because if it could, it would already have
done it, and then it would be average.

If you are the person in your field who keeps making the thing the model
can't predict — even when it's slower, even when it costs you a paycheck —
keep going. You are not redundant. You are the function that makes the
rest of the system worth running.
